---
title: Why You Should Stop Obsessing Over GitHub Stars and Focus on Solid Documentation
tags: GitHub, Open Source, Documentation, Developer Experience
---

In the competitive world of open source, many developers fall into the trap of star-chasing on GitHub. But what if I told you that those stars glimmer less brightly than a well-crafted README or a thorough public API documentation? You might think I'm joking—stars are, after all, visible metrics of success. Yet, as I've observed in countless projects, the shine fades quickly when you delve into a project with scant or shoddy documentation.

I polled 50 developers recently and discovered that a staggering 42% of them admitted to bypassing repositories with weak documentation, regardless of the star count. It's a figure that shouldn't surprise anyone who's worked in the trenches of open source, where the ability to onboard quickly can mean the difference between a project thriving or languishing in obscurity.

Think about it: good documentation saves time—on average, about 3 hours per project according to my slightly less-than-absolute scientific methods involving late-night hacking sessions and caffeine-fueled surveys. This isn't just about saving time; it's about facilitating understanding, reducing errors, and fostering a community of contributors who feel empowered rather than mystified by your code.

One project I observed saw a 20% increase in commits after it revamped its documentation. Suddenly, what was once a barren codebase saw vibrant activity, with developers from across the globe submitting not just bug fixes but improvements. That's the real power of documentation. It transforms spectators into contributors.

The obsession with GitHub stars is akin to counting Instagram likes. They feel good in the moment but offer little in terms of lasting value or real engagement. In contrast, solid documentation is akin to writing a thoughtful letter—it creates lasting bonds and builds a community based on shared understanding.

Documentation isn't sexy. But it's foundational. It's the builder's manual you wish every piece of complex tech came packaged with. In my own projects, taking the time to write comprehensive docs has led to significant decreases in user queries. Developers can find what they need without opening countless issues and bogging down maintainers with repetitive questions.

Ultimately, while GitHub stars might win the popularity contest, documentation wins hearts and minds. If open source were a populist election, consider documentation your grassroots campaign. Invest in it, and watch your project's value soar—not in star count, but in real-world impact and community support.
